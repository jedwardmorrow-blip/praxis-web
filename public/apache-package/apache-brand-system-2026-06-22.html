01 — The story

A local institution, not a startup

Apache is a family-run Tucson business built on service relationships that outlast any single product. Mail, phones, documents, shipping, proof, supplies — to a customer these aren't separate purchases. They're the daily communication infrastructure of an office, kept running by people who answer the phone and show up. The brand's job is to make that continuity legible: established enough to trust, responsive enough to still feel personal.

Apache keeps Tucson businesses connected.

Sixty-plus years in one community is the differentiator. Tell that story with restraint — it should build trust, never tip into nostalgia.

The story frame, in practice
  • Say "family-run" or "local Tucson team" when speaking generally.
  • Use "Est. 1961" or "60+ years" until exact dates are confirmed.
  • Talk about people answering, showing up, and solving problems.
  • Keep it from getting sentimental or overly founder-focused.

Story proof to gather

The team, a service vehicle, the building, an equipment install, and one honest portrait of the Apache crew.

Customer proof to gather

Same-day delivery, an equipment issue solved, a certified-mail workflow cleaned up, a phone system that follows through.

Local proof to gather

Tucson base, Southern Arizona service area, and the office categories Apache already serves well.

03 — Logo system

Two directions, one heritage

Two directions worth carrying forward. Both protect what Apache already owns — the orange, the serif name, the tagline. The Heritage Block is the safe near-term refinement; The Keystone is the evolution that adds a true symbol. They share a palette and a monogram, so moving from one to the other later is a step, not a restart.

Direction A · The Heritage Block Recommended to lead

The safest production path. Keeps the recognized orange block and serif name, drops the dated contact-line clutter, and squares the proportions. Looks like Apache — just tidied and load-bearing.

Best for: the near-term site, proposals, invoices, and everyday collateral.

Direction B · The Keystone The evolution
Apache BUSINESS SYSTEMS

A confident new mark: an "A" cut as a keystone and an upward arrow — stability plus forward motion. The most ownable and scalable option, and the one that finally gives Apache a symbol that works with no wordmark at all.

Best for: the app icon, signage, vehicle, and any moment the name can't fit.

Recommendation

Run the Heritage Block as the working identity now — it earns trust fastest and changes the least. Commission the Keystone in parallel as the symbol for places the wordmark can't go, and let it lead once it's proven. Keep the current production files live until Apache signs off on the direction.

Keystone construction

Built on a 6-unit grid. Apex on the vertical axis, 22-unit corner radius, symmetrical legs. The crossbar sits one unit below center — never recentre it.

Hold the geometry

05 — Color

Orange is equity. Navy is structure.

The 70 / 25 / 5 rule

Roughly 70% paper and neutral, 25% navy, 5% orange. Orange earns attention because it's rationed.

State accents

Green / brass / red for success, heritage notes, and alerts only — never as brand color.

Contrast

Orange on navy and pearl on navy both pass for large text. Never set orange text on paper for body copy.

07 — Voice

Plainspoken, useful, local

Apache should sound like a trusted local operator, not a vendor brochure. Lead with the outcome and the local support. Reach for technology language only when it clarifies the customer's job.

Voice traits

  • Plain and specific
  • Confident without hype
  • Service-first and local
  • Operational, not trendy
  • Helpful before promotional

Use these words

  • Local service
  • Business systems
  • Customer communications
  • Proof and tracking
  • Mail, documents, phones, shipping
  • Support, supplies, response

Avoid these words

  • Revolutionary
  • Disruptive
  • "AI-powered" as a vague claim
  • Best-in-class without proof
  • Managed everything
  • Generic "solutions"
Homepage headline
Tucson business systems, supported by people you can reach.

Follow with concrete outcomes: mailing, documents, phones, shipping, proof, and service for Southern Arizona offices.

Service page
Certified mail workflows without the manual chase.

Start with the pain, explain the workflow, name who it's for, then invite a practical conversation.

Do

"If your office depends on mail, phones, documents, notices, or shipping, Apache keeps those workflows moving — with local support."

"We deliver next-generation AI-driven digital transformation solutions for every business need."

10 — Motion & digital product

Calm, quick, never flashy

Motion should feel like good service: prompt and unobtrusive. One easing curve, short durations, fades by default. Nothing bounces, spins, or slides across the screen demanding attention.

Respect prefers-reduced-motion: drop transforms, keep instant fades.

Principles
  • Fades are the default. Slides only inside carousels.
  • One thing moves at a time. No staggered cascades over 3 items.
  • No bounce, elastic, spin, or parallax.
  • Motion confirms an action — it never decorates.

11 — Governance

Use this as a decision filter

Before anything ships, ask:

  1. Does it preserve Apache's orange/navy identity?
  2. Does it sound like a local service team, not a tech vendor?
  3. Does it name a specific workflow pain?
  4. Does it create a qualified next conversation?
  5. Are all claims approved or sourced?
  6. Is the next step obvious?
Safe to say

Family-run · local Tucson team · Est. 1961 / 60+ years · mailing, documents, phones, shipping, certified proof, supplies, and service.

Confirm before publishing

Exact years beyond "60+", customer counts, retention or market share, awards and certifications, response-time guarantees, and official social links.

Brief for AI & future builders

When building Apache collateral, preserve the existing brand equity and make the work more useful, specific, and revenue-oriented. Do not rebrand Apache into a tech startup. Do not invent claims, awards, customer counts, certifications, or years beyond the approved constants.
